# Path to Pure Mathematics

goal: analysis, number theory, algebra — classical foundations

---

## Stage 1 — Pre-University

1. **Elementary Algebra** — Hall & Knight — work every problem
2. **Plane Trigonometry** — SL Loney — same, don't skip problems
3. **Elements of Coordinate Geometry** — SL Loney
4. **Precalculus** — Israel Gelfand — functions, algebra, trig done right
5. **How to Solve It** — George Pólya — read slowly; teaches mathematical thinking
6. **A Mathematician's Apology** — G.H. Hardy — short, beautiful; read early

---

## Stage 2 — Bridge

7. **Higher Mathematics for Beginners** — Yaglom & Zeldovich — Soviet classic; bridges arithmetic and calculus
8. **What Is Mathematics?** — Richard Courant — broad and beautiful; builds real intuition
9. **Challenge and Thrill of Pre-College Mathematics**
10. **Book of Proof** — Richard Hammack — learn to read and write proofs
11. **Calculus Made Easy** — Silvanus Thompson
12. **The Essence of Calculus** — Grant Sanderson — watch before or alongside Spivak

---

## Stage 3 — Early Undergraduate

13. **Calculus: An Intuitive and Physical Approach** — Morris Kline — broad warmup before Spivak
14. **Calculus** — Michael Spivak — rigorous calculus; the real beginning
15. **Calculus with Analytic Geometry** — George Simmons — classical companion to Spivak
16. **A Course of Pure Mathematics** — G.H. Hardy — Hardy's own analysis textbook
17. **The Princeton Companion to Mathematics** — Timothy Gowers — use as reference throughout
18. **A Book of Abstract Algebra** — Charles Pinter — most accessible abstract algebra
19. **The Higher Arithmetic** — Harold Davenport — short, elegant; entry into number theory
20. **Introduction to the Theory of Numbers** — Niven, Zuckerman & Montgomery
21. **Generatingfunctionology** — Herbert Wilf
22. **Concrete Mathematics** — Knuth, Graham & Patashnik

---

## Stage 4 — Analysis & Number Theory

23. **Understanding Analysis** — Stephen Abbott — gentlest rigorous introduction
24. **How to Think About Analysis** — Lara Alcock — metacognitive companion; read when analysis gets hard
25. **Real Mathematical Analysis** — Charles Pugh — more geometric than Rudin
26. **Mathematical Analysis** — Tom Apostol — rigorous real analysis with metric spaces; bridges Pugh to analytic number theory
27. **Visual Complex Analysis** — Tristan Needham — complex analysis with geometric intuition
28. **Introduction to Analytic Number Theory** — Tom Apostol
29. **Analytic Number Theory: Exploring the Anatomy of Integers** — de Koninck & Luca
30. **An Introduction to the Theory of Infinite Series** — Bromwich
31. **Inequalities** — Hardy, Littlewood & Pólya
32. **Gamma: Exploring Euler's Constant** — Julian Havil — readable and beautiful
33. **A Course of Modern Analysis** — Whittaker & Watson — the classical reference; gateway to advanced work

---

## Stage 5 — Advanced

34. **An Introduction to the Theory of Numbers** — Hardy & Wright — monograph, not textbook; read after stage 4
35. **Disquisitiones Arithmeticae** — Gauss — number theory's founding text
36. **Elliptic Functions and Elliptic Integrals** — Prasolov & Solovyev
37. **The Applications of Elliptic Functions** — Greenhill — classical 19th-century treatment
38. **Modular Functions and Dirichlet Series** — Tom Apostol
39. **A First Course in Modular Forms** — Diamond & Shurman — standard graduate text
40. **Modular Forms: A Classical Approach** — Cohen & Strömberg

---

- **Putnam and Beyond** — Andreescu & Gelca
- **Proofs from the Book** — Aigner & Ziegler

## Context & History

- **Mathematical Thought from Ancient to Modern Times** — Morris Kline
- **The Crest of the Peacock** — George Joseph — non-European mathematics
- **A Passage to Infinity** — George Joseph — Kerala mathematics and its precalculus tradition
- **Lilavati of Bhaskaracarya** — medieval Indian mathematics, primary source
- **Journey Through Genius** — William Dunham — great theorems told as stories
- **The Calculus Gallery** — William Dunham — masterpieces from Newton to Lebesgue; shows the actual humans struggling with the ideas
- **Infinite Powers** — Steven Strogatz — narrative arc of how calculus was built and why; strong on intuition and motivation
- **Euler: The Master of Us All** — William Dunham
- **Fermat's Last Theorem** — Simon Singh — shows why all this matters
- **Hilbert** — Constance Reid

---

## Notes

- Stages 1–2 are years of solid work. Don't rush them.
- Problem books run in parallel throughout — not sequentially.
- Spivak is the real analysis warm-up; Abbott is the formal transition into rigorous analysis.
- Whittaker & Watson is the bridge from analysis to advanced number theory.
- Hardy & Wright and Gauss are not textbooks — read them after stage 4.
