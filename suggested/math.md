# Path to Ramanujan's Work

Starting point: lower primary school level, Hall & Knight algebra, SL Loney trig in hand.
Run problem books (prob/) in parallel throughout all stages — not sequentially.

## Stage 1 — Right Now

1. `elem/` — **Hall & Knight Elementary Algebra** *(not yet in collection — acquire)* — work every problem
2. `elem/` — **SL Loney Plane Trigonometry** — same, don't skip problems
3. `elem/` — **SL Loney Elements of Coordinate Geometry** — natural companion to the trig
4. `elem/` — **Precalculus — Gelfand** — excellent series; functions, coordinates, trigonometry, algebra done right
5. `gen/` — **A Synopsis of Elementary Results — George Carr** — browse, don't study. This is the only book Ramanujan had. Worth knowing.
6. `bio/` — **A Mathematician's Apology — G.H. Hardy** — not technical, short, beautiful. Hardy mentions Ramanujan directly.
7. `elem/` — **An Introduction to the Creativity of Ramanujan — PK Srinivasan** — read alongside for motivation; no pressure to understand everything
8. `elem/` — **How to Solve It — Polya** — read slowly, teaches mathematical thinking

## Stage 2 — After Algebra and Trig Are Solid

9. `elem/` — **Higher Mathematics for Beginners — Yaglom & Zeldovich** — Soviet classic; bridges arithmetic and calculus with unusual clarity
10. `gen/` — **What is Mathematics — Courant** — broad and beautiful, builds real intuition
11. `elem/` — **Challenge and Thrill of Pre-College Mathematics** — problem solving
12. `proof/` — **Book of Proof — Hammack** — learn to read and write proofs
13. `calc/` — **Calculus Made Easy — Gardner & Thompson** — first taste of calculus
14. `calc/` — **Essence of Calculus — 3Blue1Brown** — watch before or alongside Spivak; geometric intuition first
15. `bio/srinivasa-ramanujan/` — **The Man Who Knew Infinity — Robert Kanigel** — the definitive biography, not technical; read early and often
16. `bio/` — **Srinivasa Ramanujan** (other bios in the folder) — read for motivation at any point
17. `hist/` — **Fermat's Last Theorem — Simon Singh** — not technical; tells the story of how modular forms led to the proof. Shows why all this matters.

## Stage 3 — Early Undergraduate

18. `calc/` — **Calculus: An Intuitive and Physical Approach — Morris Kline** — broad, classical, strong on applications; good warmup before Spivak
19. `calc/` — **Hitchhiker's Guide to Calculus — Spivak** then **Calculus — Spivak** — rigorous calculus
20. `calc/` — **Calculus with Analytic Geometry — George Simmons** — broader and more classical, good companion to Spivak
21. `gen/` — **A Course of Pure Mathematics — G.H. Hardy** — Hardy's own analysis textbook
22. `gen/` — **The Princeton Companion to Mathematics — Timothy Gowers** — use as a reference throughout; dip in as topics arise
23. `alg/` — **A Book of Abstract Algebra — Charles Pinter** — most accessible abstract algebra
24. `nt/` — **The Higher Arithmetic — Harold Davenport** — short, elegant, very readable; good entry into number theory
25. `nt/` — **Introduction to the Theory of Numbers — Niven, Zuckerman & Montgomery** — the standard undergraduate text; read after Davenport
26. `dm/` — **Generatingfunctionology — Herb Wilf** — generating functions, the language Ramanujan thought in
27. `dm/` — **Concrete Mathematics — Knuth** — combinatorics and series

## Stage 4 — Intermediate

28. `anal/` — **Understanding Analysis — Stephen Abbott** — the gentlest rigorous introduction to real analysis
29. `anal/` — **How to Think About Analysis — Lara Alcock** — metacognitive companion; read it when analysis gets hard
30. `anal/` — **Real Mathematical Analysis — Charles Pugh** — rigorous real analysis, more geometric than Rudin; read after Abbott
31. `anal/` — **Visual Complex Analysis — Tristan Needham** — complex analysis with geometric intuition
32. `nt/` — **Introduction to Analytic Number Theory — Tom Apostol**
33. `nt/` — **Analytic Number Theory: Exploring the Anatomy of Integers — de Koninck & Luca** — good second course alongside Apostol
34. `anal/` — **An Introduction to the Theory of Infinite Series — Bromwich** — Ramanujan's series work becomes readable here
35. `anal/` — **Inequalities — Hardy, Littlewood & Pólya** — inequalities are everywhere in Ramanujan's work; Hardy and Littlewood knew Ramanujan personally
36. `anal/` — **Gamma: Exploring Euler's Constant — Havil & Dyson** — the gamma function runs through Ramanujan's notebooks; readable and beautiful
37. `anal/` — **Whittaker & Watson — A Course of Modern Analysis** — the book Ramanujan himself used. Read after Needham and Apostol.
38. `nt/` — **The Theory of Partitions — George Andrews** — directly into Ramanujan territory
39. `nt/` — **Number Theory in the Spirit of Ramanujan — Bruce Berndt**
40. `bio/srinivasa-ramanujan/` — **My Search for Ramanujan — Ken Ono** — personal and motivating; a modern mathematician following Ramanujan's path
41. `bio/` — **Littlewood's Miscellany** — Littlewood was Hardy's collaborator and knew Ramanujan. Fun at any point.

## Stage 5 — Advanced

42. `anal/` — **Elliptic Functions and Elliptic Integrals — Prasolov & Solovyev** — elliptic functions are central to Ramanujan's later work
43. `anal/` — **The Applications of Elliptic Functions — Greenhill** — classical 19th-century treatment; the tradition Ramanujan worked in
44. `nt/` — **An Introduction to the Theory of Numbers — Hardy & Wright** — monograph, not textbook; read after stage 4
45. `nt/` — **Disquisitiones Arithmeticae — Gauss** — number theory's founding text; Ramanujan worked in this tradition
46. `nt/` — **Modular Functions and Dirichlet Series — Tom Apostol**
47. `nt/` — **A First Course in Modular Forms — Diamond & Shurman** — standard graduate text
48. `nt/` — **Modular Forms: A Classical Approach — Cohen & Stromberg**
49. `nt/` — **Development of Elliptic Functions According to Ramanujan — Cooper & Venkatachaliengar**
50. `anal/` — **Pi and the AGM — Jonathan & Peter Borwein** — analytic number theory and computational complexity; Ramanujan's pi formulas
51. `bio/srinivasa-ramanujan/` — **Ramanujan: Twelve Lectures — G.H. Hardy** — Hardy's own account of Ramanujan's mathematics; reads differently once you have the background

## Stage 6 — Primary Sources

52. `anal/` — **Ramanujan's Notebooks — Bruce Berndt** (5 volumes) — Berndt's annotation of Ramanujan's actual notebooks
53. `anal/` — **Ramanujan's Lost Notebook — Andrews & Berndt** — the 1976 discovery
54. `anal/` — **The Continued Fractions in the Unorganized Portions of Ramanujan's Notebooks — Andrews, Berndt et al.** — deep dive into one specific area
55. `bio/srinivasa-ramanujan/` — **Collected Papers of Ramanujan — Aiyar, Hardy & Wilson** — the primary source: everything Ramanujan published in his lifetime

## Cultural & Historical Context — Read Whenever

Not on the technical path but add depth:

- `hist/` — **The Crest of the Peacock — George Joseph** — non-European mathematics; the Indian tradition that shaped the soil Ramanujan grew in
- `hist/` — **A Passage to Infinity — George Joseph** — specifically on Kerala mathematics and its precalculus tradition
- `hist/` — **Lilavati of Bhaskaracarya** — medieval Indian mathematics, primary source
- `bio/` — **Journey Through Genius — William Dunham** — great theorems told as stories; motivating at any stage
- `bio/` — **Euler: The Master of Us All — Dunham** and **Leonard Euler: Life, Work and Legacy** — Euler's style is the closest historical analogue to Ramanujan's
- `bio/` — **A Tribute to Paul Erdős** and **The Man Who Loved Only Numbers** — mathematical culture
- `bio/` — **Hilbert — Constance Reid** — to understand the world Ramanujan entered in 1913

## Notes

- Stages 1–2 are years of work done well. Don't rush them.
- The Carr synopsis and Hardy's Apology are the two most Ramanujan-specific books readable right now.
- Gelfand's Precalculus and Loney's Coordinate Geometry (Stage 1) build exactly the foundations Ramanujan himself had.
- Whittaker & Watson sits between stages 4 and 5 — reading it carries particular meaning since Ramanujan used it.
- Inequalities (Hardy-Littlewood-Pólya) and the elliptic functions books (Prasolov, Greenhill) are essential for understanding what Ramanujan was actually doing in his notebooks.
- The lost notebook was discovered by Andrews in 1976 in the Trinity College library. It took decades to work through. That's where the path ends — and where research begins.
